## What Velo is

Velo is a **dental practice management SaaS** built for **independent dental clinics in Iraq**, with WhatsApp-first patient communication and Arabic-first design.

It is sold and managed by SupCod3 as an operator-led service: clinics do not self-onboard. The operator (currently Ali) provisions each clinic, configures their integrations (WhatsApp credentials, AI quotas), and supports them directly. This is a deliberate choice, not a temporary limitation.

---

## Why Velo exists

**The problem:**

Independent dental clinics in Iraq run on paper, Excel, and informal WhatsApp threads. The available alternatives are:

- **Western dental software** (Curve, Dentrix, Open Dental) — built for US/EU billing systems, English-only, $200-500/month, no WhatsApp, no Arabic, no IQD currency support, and require credit cards or US bank accounts to even sign up.
- **Generic CRMs** (Zoho, HubSpot) — not dental-specific, no tooth charts, no treatment plans, no procedure-level billing.
- **Regional MENA tools** — most target Egypt/UAE markets with insurance-claim flows that don't apply to Iraq's mostly-cash dental market.
- **Nothing** — which is what 90%+ of Iraqi dental clinics actually use.

The result: a typical Iraqi dental clinic loses patients (no recall system), under-collects revenue (no treatment plan tracking), and burns receptionist time (manual scheduling, paper records).

**Why now:**

- WhatsApp has near-universal penetration in Iraq (~95% smartphone owners). It's the primary patient communication channel — far more than email or SMS.
- Iraqi private healthcare is growing as the middle class expands and oil-economy stability returns.
- Cloud SaaS infrastructure (Supabase, Vercel) makes it economically viable to serve small clinics at $30-100/month price points that were impossible a decade ago.
- AI-powered features (treatment plan summaries, patient WhatsApp triage) make a small clinic feel like a large one — without hiring more staff.

## Who it's for

### Primary user persona — Dr. Saif (the owner-dentist)

- 30-50 years old
- Runs a small clinic (1-3 chairs, 2-5 staff total)
- Owns the practice AND is the lead dentist
- Communicates in Arabic with patients, English/Arabic mix with staff
- Lives on WhatsApp; checks email rarely
- Has a smartphone (Android, mid-range) and a laptop (often shared with reception)
- Manages everything: bookings, billing, supplier payments, marketing
- Pain: forgets follow-ups, loses patients to competitors, doesn't know which treatments are profitable
- **Goal: more revenue per existing patient, fewer no-shows, less time on admin**

### Secondary user persona — Sara (the receptionist)

- 22-35 years old
- Front desk: phone, walk-ins, scheduling, payment collection
- Heavy WhatsApp user — books appointments through it, sends reminders, answers questions
- May handle 30-50 patient interactions per day
- Pain: juggling appointments on paper, forgetting payments, getting overwhelmed when both phones ring
- **Goal: see today's schedule at a glance, send a reminder in 2 clicks, take payment without errors**

### Tertiary persona — The hygienist or dental assistant

- Read-mostly user
- Looks up patient history, sees what the dentist marked on the chart, prepares the room
- **Goal: know what's coming next**

### Not the target

- Hospital dental departments (different procurement cycle, different software needs)
- Multi-location dental groups (10+ clinics) — different problem entirely
- Dental schools / academic clinics
- Insurance-heavy markets (Saudi Arabia, UAE expat clinics) where claim management dominates the workflow
- US/EU clinics — they have established players and very different regulatory/billing realities

## The four pillars

Every feature in Velo must serve at least one of these four pillars. If a feature doesn't fit any of them, it doesn't ship.

### Pillar 1: Clinical workflow that matches how Iraqi dentists actually work

- **Patient records** with medical history, allergies, demographics
- **Anatomical dental chart** (FDI numbering, real tooth shapes, surface-level findings)
- **Treatment plans** with line-item procedures, costs, status tracking
- **Appointment scheduling** with multi-doctor support, conflict detection, status workflow
- Bilingual UI (English + Arabic, RTL-aware)

### Pillar 2: WhatsApp-first patient communication

- **Outbound messaging** — appointment confirmations, reminders, payment receipts
- **Inbound** — patients reply, clinic sees it, can respond
- **Automated reminders** — 24h and 2h before appointment
- **AI triage** — patients ask questions in Arabic, AI responds with clinic-defined answers

### Pillar 3: Money that makes sense for Iraqi cash dentistry

- **Multi-currency** — IQD as primary, USD for higher-end procedures
- **No insurance complexity** — every payment is recorded, every payment is paid
- **Treatment plan → payment linkage** — see how much each plan has been paid against
- **Revenue reports** — daily, monthly, by doctor, by procedure
- **Expense tracking** — basic enough not to need separate accounting software for a small clinic

### Pillar 4: The operator handles the hard parts

- **Onboarding** — operator provisions accounts; clinics don't sign up themselves
- **Credential management** — operator configures WhatsApp Business API, AI keys, currency settings
- **Support** — operator has impersonation access to debug and assist
- **Quotas and limits** — operator manages rate limits per clinic transparently
- **Billing** — handled outside the app (invoicing, contracts) for now

- ✅ Inventory (basic stock tracking, low-stock alerts)
- ✅ Basic reports (revenue, appointments, top procedures)
- ✅ Tasks (assign to team members)
- ✅ Settings (clinic info, doctors list, team roles)
- ✅ Bilingual EN/AR with RTL support
- 🚧 **Anatomical dental chart visual** (Sprint 1 — currently functional but ugly)
- 🚧 **Operator console with impersonation** (built, browser-test in Sprint 1)
- 🚧 **WhatsApp send + reminders** (API built, end-to-end test in Sprint 2)

### Out of scope until validated

- Insurance/claims management
- Patient-facing portal or app
- Lab work / external referral tracking
- Multi-location clinic groups
- Dental imaging integration (panoramic, CBCT, DICOM)
- Inventory purchase orders
- Advanced reporting (cohort analysis, retention curves, demographic breakdowns)
- Payroll / staff hours
- Marketing automation beyond appointment reminders
- Public website / clinic landing pages

### Explicitly never in scope

- Generic CRM features (deals, leads, sales pipelines, ticket queues)
- Cross-industry abstraction (we are NOT building a "general practice management" tool)
- Self-serve clinic signup flow (the operator-managed model is intentional)

## What success looks like

### 30 days after first customer onboarded (Dr. Saif)

- Daily active use by Dr. Saif and his receptionist (>3 sessions/day per user)
- All new patients enter through Velo (not on paper)
- All new appointments scheduled in Velo (not WhatsApp threads)
- Dental chart is being filled in for every visit
- At least one treatment plan created and tracked through to payment
- Zero P0 bugs blocking workflow
- Dr. Saif gives explicit feedback on what's missing/broken (used to plan Sprint 2)

### 90 days

- Dr. Saif's clinic data has 100+ patients in the system
- Recall system identifies 10+ patients overdue for cleaning
- Revenue reports match Dr. Saif's mental model of the month's income
- Dr. Saif is comfortable enough to refer Velo to one peer
- One more clinic onboarded as a result of the referral
- Pricing decided based on observed willingness-to-pay

### 6 months

- 5-10 clinics paying
- Average clinic uses Velo for >70% of patient interactions
- WhatsApp reminders reduce no-show rate by measurable amount (clinic self-reported is fine for now)
- Operator can onboard a new clinic in <2 hours of total work (provisioning + walkthrough)
- Recurring monthly revenue covers Supabase + Vercel + Anthropic costs with margin

### 12 months

- 25-50 clinics paying
- Velo is the default recommended tool among private dental clinics in Erbil/Baghdad
- Profitable enough to support Ali full-time OR support hiring one technical person
- Decision point: stay Iraq-only, or expand to KRG-adjacent markets (Jordan, Lebanon)

## Non-functional requirements

### Performance

- Page load <3 seconds on a mid-range Android tablet over Iraqi 3G
- Bundle size <500KB main chunk gzipped (currently 442KB ✓)
- Database queries <500ms p95 on Supabase free tier
- Image-heavy pages (dental chart, eventually x-rays) lazy-load

### Security

- Multi-tenant via RLS — every query scoped by `org_id`
- Defense-in-depth: helpers verify org_id again client-side
- All money writes audit-logged with operator-impersonation trail
- No client-side API keys (Anthropic, WhatsApp routed through server proxies)
- All file storage signed-URL gated (when implemented)
- Service role key only used in `api/*` Vercel functions, never in browser
- Test accounts limited (50 patients, 100 appointments, 14-day expiry)

### Compliance

- No HIPAA — Iraqi market is not subject to US healthcare regulation
- Iraqi medical-records norms are informal; clinic owns its data; we provide export
- GDPR not applicable for Iraqi clinics, but design with data-export-ready in mind
- Right to deletion: clinic can request full data export + account deletion at any time

### Reliability

- Supabase Pro tier when revenue justifies (monthly backups, point-in-time recovery)
- Vercel deploys on push to main; preview deploys on every PR
- Audit log captures every mutation; if audit log fails, app surfaces a toast (so operator knows trail is incomplete)
- No client-side data persistence beyond auth session and a few UI preferences

### Observability

- Vercel logs for serverless function errors
- Supabase logs for database errors
- Application audit_log table for all mutations
- No third-party error tracking yet (Sentry, LogRocket) — add when scale justifies

## Constraints and decisions

### Why operator-managed (not self-serve)

The Iraqi dental market is too small and too relationship-driven to justify a self-serve funnel. Clinic owners trust people, not signup forms. The operator (Ali) is the human relationship that converts trust into a paying account. This also means:
- Lower customer acquisition cost per clinic (relational sales)
- Higher customer lifetime value (operator support reduces churn)
- Slower scale (operator is a bottleneck — but that's fine for the first 50 clinics)

### Why dental-only

Generic dental + GP + chiropractic + cosmetic = a worse product for everyone. Pivot away from generic CRM was a hard decision but the right one. The product can talk to dentists with confidence: "yes, FDI numbering, yes treatment plans, yes dental chart with surfaces." Generic competitors can't.

### Why Iraq first

You're physically in Erbil. Your network is here. The market is uncontested. Local language, currency, and culture are deeply understood. Expanding before nailing one market is the indie-SaaS death sentence.

### Why no insurance flows

Iraq is overwhelmingly cash dentistry. Building insurance-claim flows for a market that doesn't have meaningful private dental insurance is wasted complexity. If/when KRG private insurance for dentistry expands, we revisit.

### Why no patient app

Iraqi patients communicate through WhatsApp. Asking them to download a separate app to see their next appointment is friction they won't accept. WhatsApp is the patient app.

## Pricing (TBD — decision deferred to Sprint 2)

Open question. Some reference points:

- **Local context**: Iraqi clinics typically generate $5K-30K/month revenue depending on size. Software at $30-50/month is well within tolerance for the lower end; $100-200/month for the upper end if it visibly drives revenue.
- **Comparable markets**: Egyptian dental software (Drsync, Vetra) prices around $30-80/month. Egyptian clinics are similar economically to mid-tier Iraqi clinics.
- **Operator cost**: Each clinic costs you maybe $5-10/month in Supabase + Vercel + AI quota at current usage. Need at least 5x that to be sustainable.

**Working hypothesis**: $50/month per clinic, billed quarterly in IQD or USD. Revisit after Dr. Saif uses Velo for 60 days and gives explicit feedback on whether $50/month feels reasonable to him.

**Anti-pattern to avoid**: Free indefinitely. A clinic that pays nothing values nothing. Plan to convert Dr. Saif from free trial to paid at the 90-day mark.

## Risks and mitigations

### Risk 1 — Dr. Saif uses it for 30 days, hates the dental chart visual, churns

**Mitigation:** Sprint 1 priority #3 is the dental chart visual rebuild. Ship it before Dr. Saif starts.

### Risk 2 — WhatsApp Business API approval is harder than expected

**Mitigation:** Start the WhatsApp Business approval process in parallel with Sprint 1 work. If it takes 4 weeks, that's 4 weeks of shipping other features in the meantime.

### Risk 3 — Operator flow has bugs that only show up under impersonation load

**Mitigation:** Sprint 1 priority #1 + #2 are operator-flow testing. Find bugs before customers do.

### Risk 4 — Ali burns out trying to do everything (dev + sales + support)

**Mitigation:** Pricing converts to revenue. Revenue funds either part-time help or full-time independence. Don't onboard customers faster than the operator can support them. Hard cap at 5 clinics for the first 90 days.

### Risk 5 — Iraqi political/economic instability disrupts the market

**Mitigation:** Outside your control. SaaS revenue is geographically distributable; once one clinic is profitable, the model itself is portable to Lebanon, Jordan, KRG-adjacent markets if needed.

### Risk 6 — A bigger player (Iraqi tech firm, MENA SaaS) launches dental software

**Mitigation:** First-mover relational selling beats anonymous self-serve in this market. Your moat is the operator relationship, not the software. Software is a commodity; trust isn't.

## Open product questions

These are decisions that don't need to be answered today but will need answers within 90 days:

1. **Multi-location clinics** — what happens if Dr. Saif opens a second branch? Currently no multi-org concept on a single profile. (Defer until needed.)
2. **Patient deduplication** — if the same patient visits two clinics that both use Velo, are they one patient or two? Privacy says two; UX says one. Likely two for now.
3. **Lab integration** — when Dr. Saif sends a crown to a lab, does Velo track that? Probably yes, eventually.
4. **Inventory purchasing** — do clinics want Velo to track supplier orders, or is that better left to WhatsApp + paper?
5. **Pricing model evolution** — flat per-clinic, or tiered by patient volume / staff count?
6. **Localization beyond Iraq** — at what point do we add Iraqi Dinar fil precision (for 250 IQD denominations)?
